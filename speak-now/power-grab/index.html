POWER GRAB

Federal Grants · 2 CFR 200
OMB is rewriting the rules for $1.1 trillion in federal grants. Match each change to what it does — then comment before July 13.
SOURCE: FEDERAL REGISTER · 91 FR 32198 · E.O. 14332
We do the research. You solve the puzzle.
It's the biggest rewrite of how federal grant money works in over a decade — $1.1 trillion a year, across 40-plus agencies. Critics call it a power grab: it hands whoever holds the White House new control over who gets the money. You can comment until July 13.

What Does It Actually Do?
Change A
Before any discretionary grant goes out, a senior political appointee must sign off — and the rule bars them from simply deferring to the expert peer reviewers. Politics gets the last word over the science.
Match a change here
✓ Federal Register — Regulation for Federal Financial Assistance (91 FR 32198) ✓ Faegre Drinker — pre-issuance political review
Change B
Agencies could cancel an active grant whenever they decide it no longer fits "agency priorities" or "the national interest" — no finding of wrongdoing, and the rule says they need not offer hearings or appeals.
Match a change here
✓ Federal Register — § 200.340, termination ✓ Crowell & Moring — Grants Overhauled
Change C
The rulebook used to call itself flexible "guidance," not binding law. This rewrite swaps in "regulations" across hundreds of provisions — making the terms of $1.1 trillion in grants binding, without a vote in Congress.
Match a change here
✓ Federal Register — guidance reissued as regulation ✓ Hogan Lovells — Federal Grantmaking Transformed
Change D
Federal awards may no longer fund what the rule calls "gender ideology" or DEI activities — and falling short is treated as a "material breach" that can trigger termination.
Match a change here
✓ Federal Register — prohibited activities ✓ Inside Government Contracts — content restrictions
