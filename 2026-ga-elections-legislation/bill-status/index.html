Reading a Bill's Status

What does "Senate Read and Referred" actually mean? A plain-language guide to the legislative jargon on every Georgia bill.
Georgia General Assembly · Editorial Reference

Every Georgia bill carries a status — "Hopper," "Read and Referred," "Withdrawn, Recommitted," "Engrossed." These aren't decorative. Each one tells you exactly where a bill is in the process, and what its chances are. Here's how to read them.

🗺️ Life of a Bill

A simplified flow. Most bills die before reaching step 5.

  1. Filed

    A legislator drafts a bill (often with the Office of Legislative Counsel) and files it with the Clerk of the House or Secretary of the Senate. Status: HOPPER or PREFILE.

  2. Introduced

    The bill's title is read aloud on the chamber floor and assigned to a standing committee. Status: READ AND REFERRED.

  3. Committee considers it

    The committee may hold hearings, accept amendments, write a substitute, or do nothing at all. Most bills die right here.

  4. Reported to the floor

    If the committee approves, the bill heads to the full chamber for debate and a vote. Status: COMMITTEE FAVORABLY REPORTED.

  5. Floor vote

    The chamber debates, possibly amends, and votes. Status: PASSAGE or LOST.

  6. Crossover Day

    Day 30 of the 40-day session. A bill must pass at least one chamber by this date or it generally cannot pass that year.

  7. Other chamber repeats steps 2–5

    If the second chamber changes the bill, the originating chamber must agree — or the bill goes to a conference committee.

  8. Sent to Governor

    The governor has 40 days to sign, veto, or do nothing. In Georgia, doing nothing means the bill becomes law automatically.

🌱Where Bills Begin

Before a bill can be debated, it has to be filed and formally introduced. These are the earliest status terms you'll see.

Hopper
What it is

The symbolic location where a filed bill waits to be officially read on the chamber floor.

▶ What it usually signals

The bill exists on paper but hasn't yet entered the formal legislative process. It's on the runway, not in the air.

Prefile
What it is

A bill filed before the legislative session officially begins (between November and the second Monday of January).

▶ What it usually signals

Sponsors are organized and ready. Prefiled bills are typically heard earlier in the session than late filings.

First Readers / First Reading
What it is

The bill's title is read aloud in the originating chamber. Required by the Georgia Constitution — every bill must be read on three different days.

▶ What it usually signals

Procedural mile marker. The bill is officially in motion, but no decisions have been made yet.

Read and Referred
What it is

The bill has been read on the floor and assigned to a standing committee for review.

▶ What it usually signals

The journey has officially started — but the bill is now parked in committee waiting for the chair to decide its fate. Most bills never leave this stage.

📋Committee Stages

Standing committees are where most bills live and die. The chair controls whether a bill gets a hearing — and that single decision often determines whether it ever sees a floor vote.

House Second Readers
What it is

In the House, the Clerk reads the bill's title a second time on the legislative day after committee assignment. Procedural.

▶ What it usually signals

Another procedural checkpoint. The bill is moving through formalities but hasn't been substantively considered yet.

Senate Read Second Time
What it is

The Senate equivalent of "Second Readers" — the bill is read a second time on the legislative day following the committee report.

▶ What it usually signals

A bill at this stage has likely cleared committee. Real progress toward a floor vote.

Hold Bill
What it is

The committee took no formal action on the bill — neither approving nor rejecting it.

▶ What it usually signals

Death by inaction. If the committee never returns to it, the bill dies at the end of the session or biennium. The most common way bills are killed.

Committee Favorably Reported
What it is

The committee voted to recommend the bill for floor consideration in the full chamber.

▶ What it usually signals

A major hurdle cleared. Most bills die in committee — getting reported out alive is meaningful progress toward passage.

Committee Favorably Reported By Substitute
What it is

The committee approved an alternate version of the bill ("substitute," often shortened to "Comm Sub") rather than the original.

▶ What it usually signals

The bill survived but in altered form. Substitutes can strengthen, weaken, or fundamentally redirect what the original sponsor proposed.

Recommitted
What it is

A bill is sent back to a committee — either the same one or a different one — for further study.

▶ What it usually signals

Either a strategic rework or a soft kill. Bills sent back to committee often don't return to the floor.

🏛️Floor Action

Once a bill leaves committee, the full chamber can debate, amend, and vote. This is where bills become law — or fail publicly.

Engrossed (Senate)
What it is

A motion in the Senate to lock the bill's text. If two-thirds vote to engross, no amendments are allowed during floor debate.

▶ What it usually signals

Sponsors want to push the bill through fast and unaltered. Often used for politically sensitive legislation.

Third Readers / Third Reading
What it is

The bill's third and final reading before a floor vote. The Georgia Constitution requires three readings in each chamber.

▶ What it usually signals

A floor vote is imminent. This is when the chamber decides yes or no.

Passage / Adopted
What it is

A majority of the chamber voted yes. The bill passes that chamber.

▶ What it usually signals

Real progress — but the bill still has to clear the other chamber, possibly survive a conference committee, and get the governor's signature.

Passage By Substitute
What it is

The chamber passed an altered version of the bill — the substitute — rather than the original.

▶ What it usually signals

The bill moves on, but the originating chamber will need to agree to the changes or send it to a conference committee.

Lost
What it is

A floor vote failed — the bill did not get a majority.

▶ What it usually signals

Defeat. Reconsideration is procedurally possible but rare. Usually the end of the road for that session.

🔄Cross-Chamber Movement

A bill must pass both the House and the Senate in identical form to become law. This is where many bills die in the second half of the session.

Crossover Day
What it is

Day 30 of Georgia's 40-day legislative session. The procedural deadline by which a bill must pass at least one chamber to remain viable that session.

▶ What it usually signals

Hard deadline. If a bill hasn't crossed by Day 30, it generally cannot pass that year. Georgia's most consequential procedural date — except Sine Die.

Im Transmit
What it is

"Immediately Transmit" — the chamber that just passed the bill sends it to the other chamber right away rather than waiting.

▶ What it usually signals

Urgency. Sponsors want to keep momentum and avoid procedural delays.

House (or Senate) Insisted
What it is

The originating chamber refuses to accept changes the other chamber made to the bill.

▶ What it usually signals

Stalemate brewing. If neither chamber gives in, the bill goes to a conference committee — or dies waiting for one to be appointed.

Disagreed
What it is

The originating chamber refused to accept changes from the other chamber.

▶ What it usually signals

A conference committee is the next step — three legislators from each chamber try to negotiate a compromise version.

Agree to House (or Senate) Substitute
What it is

The originating chamber accepts the changes the other chamber made.

▶ What it usually signals

The two chambers are aligned. The bill is on its way to the governor.

⚠️How Bills Stall or Die

Most bills don't pass. They die quietly through procedural maneuvers, expired deadlines, or simple inaction. These are the terms that mean trouble.

Tabled
What it is

The bill was removed from active floor consideration along with any pending motions. It can be brought back later via a "motion to take from the table."

▶ What it usually signals

Procedural pause. Sometimes a strategic delay; sometimes quiet euthanasia. Tabled bills often stay tabled.

Taken From Table
What it is

A previously tabled bill has been restored to active consideration on the floor.

▶ What it usually signals

Resurrection. Someone wants this bill back in play — usually because circumstances or vote counts changed.

Withdrawn, Recommitted
What it is

The bill was pulled from the floor and sent back to committee.

▶ What it usually signals

Either a tactical rework or a soft kill. Most bills sent back this way don't return to the floor.

Stalled
What it is

Informal term for a bill that has stopped moving — no committee hearing, no floor schedule, no action.

▶ What it usually signals

Likely dead unless something changes. A stalled bill at end-of-session is effectively killed by the calendar.

📜The End of the Road

A bill's final stages — whether becoming law or dying for good.

Sent to Governor
What it is

Both chambers passed identical versions. The bill is now on the governor's desk for signature, veto, or inaction.

▶ What it usually signals

One step from becoming law. The governor has 40 days to act.

Effective Date
What it is

The date the law actually takes effect — often July 1 in Georgia, unless the bill specifies otherwise.

▶ What it usually signals

The bill is law. The fight is over — at least until repeal or amendment.

Veto
What it is

The governor formally rejected the bill.

▶ What it usually signals

The legislature must override with a two-thirds vote in each chamber to make it law. Override votes are rare and usually fail.

Sine Die
What it is

Latin for "without a day." The official end of a legislative session, with no date set to reconvene.

▶ What it usually signals

Session over. Bills not passed are paused (after year one) or killed for good (after year two — see below).

End of Biennium
What it is

The end of Georgia's two-year legislative cycle. Happens at Sine Die of year two (e.g., April 2026 ended the 2025–2026 biennium).

▶ What it usually signals

Hard reset. Every unfinished bill dies for good. To revive any of them, a legislator must re-introduce them from scratch in the next biennium with a new bill number.
